👁️ THE CUE — The Trigger
The cue is the signal that initiates the habit. It tells your brain "a reward is coming." Cues are usually time, place, emotional state, people, or a preceding action.
Good habit cue example: You put your gym shoes by the door every night. Seeing them in the morning is the cue that triggers your morning workout habit.
Bad habit cue example: You sit down on the couch (cue) → immediately reach for your phone and open Instagram. The couch is the cue.
How to use this: To build a habit — make the cue obvious. Put your journal on your pillow. Set a specific alarm. Link the new habit to an existing one. To break a habit — make the cue invisible. Delete the app. Put the snacks out of sight.
💭 THE CRAVING — The Desire
The craving is the motivational force behind every habit. You don't crave the habit itself — you crave the change in state it provides. You don't crave a cigarette — you crave the relief from stress. You don't crave gaming — you crave the feeling of escape.
The craving is the feeling — not the behavior. Understanding this is the key to changing habits. You can satisfy the same craving with a different response.
How to use this: Make good habits attractive. Pair the habit with something you like. For bad habits — identify the craving underneath. What feeling are you actually seeking? Then find a better way to get it.
⚡ THE RESPONSE — The Habit Itself
The response is the actual habit you perform. It can be a thought or an action. The response depends on how motivated you are and how much friction exists. The harder it is to do something, the less likely you are to do it.
Reduce friction for good habits: Set your gym bag out the night before. Put your book on your desk. Prep your meals on Sunday. Every step you remove makes it easier to act.
Add friction for bad habits: Log out of social media. Delete addictive apps and force yourself to reinstall each time. Put your phone in another room at night.
🏆 THE REWARD — The Payoff
The reward ends the habit loop and teaches the brain whether the loop is worth remembering. Rewards satisfy the craving and teach the brain what works. Good habits often have delayed rewards — this is why they're hard to build.
The problem with bad habits: They provide immediate reward and delayed cost. Junk food, scrolling, substances — they feel good immediately. The damage is later.
The problem with good habits: Working out, saving money, studying — delayed reward. This is why immediate reward stacking helps. Track your habits visibly so you feel the win today.
How to use this: Make good habits immediately satisfying. Use a habit tracker. Celebrate the small win. Never miss twice — the recovery is part of the reward loop.
